# You Don't Know You Lost

*April 2026 · 8 min read · Strategic Finance*

---

Last month, Anthropic ran an experiment inside its own company. Sixty-nine employees. A hundred dollars each. A Slack-based marketplace where AI agents negotiated on behalf of every participant — autonomously, without asking for permission.

186 deals closed. Over $4,000 in real transactions. Physical goods changed hands at the end.

The finding that should concern every executive reading this: Claude Opus significantly outperformed Claude Haiku in negotiations. The people whose agents lost didn't know. They rated the fairness of their transactions just as highly as the winners.

That's not a UX finding. That's a new category of risk.

---

## What "Invisible" Actually Means

When you lose a negotiation in the traditional sense, you know. You made an offer, they countered, you either closed the gap or walked. There's a signal.

When your agent loses, there's no signal. The deal closes. The goods arrive. Everyone reports that it went fine. You have no reference point for what a better agent would have gotten you — so the loss never registers.

Oxford University's Internet Institute formalised this in their October 2025 "Agentic Inequality" paper. Buyers using weaker agents paid approximately 2% more than those with superior agents. Weaker seller agents lost up to 14% in profit compared to negotiations between equally capable agents.

The scale compounds. If your procurement organisation runs thousands of negotiations per year, a 2% disadvantage is not a rounding error. It's a budget line.

The paper names this cleanly: agentic inequality — defined not by access to the internet but by access to the right model.

## Where It's Already Happening

### B2B Procurement

This is the most validated case, and the most immediately consequential for anyone running a P&L.

Coupa, SAP Ariba, and Jaggaer are all deploying AI agents for sourcing, pricing negotiation, and SLA terms. So are their enterprise customers. Both sides of the table now have agents. McKinsey puts the efficiency gain from autonomous procurement agents at 15–30%. By end of 2026, 60–70% of transactional procurement will be handled by AI agents.

The question your CPO cannot yet answer: what model is the supplier's agent running? And is yours better?

If the answer is "we don't know," that's the gap. Your procurement team is reporting green across the board — and the McKinsey math suggests they may be leaving 15–30% on the table relative to what a better-deployed agent would recover.

### Contract Negotiation

Ironclad, Spellbook, and DocuSign AI are live on both sides of enterprise deals. Legal teams are using AI to draft, redline, and negotiate terms. So are their counterparties.

The inequality here isn't measured in percentage points — it's measured in clauses.

The limitation of liability cap your agent accepted. The auto-renewal term your agent didn't flag. The data residency carve-out your agent missed. These are not hypothetical future risks. They are consequences embedded in contracts being signed today, by organisations whose legal AI is measurably less capable than their counterparties'.

### The Asymmetric Case: Financial Services

B2B procurement and contract negotiation are symmetric — both sides have agents. Financial services is asymmetric, which makes it sharper.

Lenders and insurers have been deploying sophisticated pricing AI for years, trained on millions of prior transactions. The consumer-facing tools are basic by comparison.

You're not being outcompeted by another consumer with a better agent. You're being outcompeted by a corporate model that has run this negotiation a hundred thousand times.

## The Human Version

None of this requires autonomous agents to feel real. Consider hiring.

The candidate sitting across from your recruiter has already used Claude to research your comp bands, identify equity refresh patterns from public filings, prep their negotiation strategy, and draft their counter. Your offer team hasn't updated its playbook in three years.

That's not agent-vs-agent. But it's the same dynamic: one side brought better intelligence to the table, and the other side doesn't know what they conceded.

---

## The Honest Take

An international autonomous negotiation competition ran 180,000 negotiations across 286 participants from 40 countries. Their most counterintuitive finding: warmth — positivity, gratitude, question-asking — outperformed aggression across every metric. Dominance strategies led to impasses. The best-performing agents didn't fight harder. They were more capable.

That's the frame for the board conversation. This is not about deploying AI to be more aggressive in negotiations. It's about deploying the right model — and understanding what model is on the other side of the table.

In Project Deal, the gap between Opus and Haiku wasn't visible from the outside. Both closed deals. Both produced satisfied participants. The difference showed up only in the aggregate outcomes — and only because Anthropic could see both sides of every transaction.

In your business, you only see one side.

The enterprises that start measuring this — what model is representing us, what model is representing them, what's the outcome delta — will close the invisible gap. The ones that don't will keep reporting green quarters, unaware that their agent keeps finishing second.
